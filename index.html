Food Insecurity In The US

Analyzing the current food access issue in America, its impacts, and what we can do.

What is Food Insecurity?

Food insecurity is a state of limited food access. Food insecurity is having no available, nutritious, or affordable food.


Food accessibility at its core is nutrition and affordability but it is also a paradox:
If food is affordable, it may not be nutritious enough to ensure a balanced diet.
If food is nutrient-dense, high prices lead to insufficient quantities of food. *

*For example, fast-food and convenience stores may be available and affordable, but, they lack fresh produce, are highly processed, and often contain too much sodium or sugar.


So, what is food insecurity? Food insecurity is scary.

Furthermore, food insecurity is not a one-dimensional issue. It is not simply an issue of food access. In telling the convoluted narrative of food insecurity we must examine race, health, poverty, education, and more, Let's delve deeper...


Who is Affected?

In the United States alone, there are 34 million individuals, including 9 million children, who are food insecure.

From urban cities to rural countryside, counties all over America experience food insecurity.

On the map, we can see that there is a level of insecurity in every county. Also, many counties have individuals living more than 10 miles from a grocery store. For perspective, if a grocery store were 10 miles away, this would require walking for 3 hours continuously. We define this as low access to a grocery store.

Within America, this is a prevalent issue among many communities and isn't going away anytime soon. Food insecurity is highest for single-mother households and below-poverty-line households. It also affects a wide range such as urban cities, rural areas, and college students.



When comparing population density with the distribution of low access to a grocery store, we can see that areas with lower population density have greater low access to a grocery store and higher population density have lower low access to a grocery store.

We considered transportation, such as having a car, as an exploratory predictor variable for food insecurity. From the data, we observed that for every county, there is less than 1% of indivudals who do not have a car. Comparatively to countries like China, the United States is less densely populated. If we observe the population density map, we notice that majority of the US is not densely populated, with the exceptions of urban areas, which suggests that majority of those who live the US must have a car to get around. Public transportation is also not very developed outside urban areas, thus cars is a must to live in the US.

When you are an individual who lives greater than 10 miles away from a grocery store, it equates up to 15 to 30 minute drive to the store. Individual takes around 45 mins to 1 hour to shop, resulting in a total of almost 2 hours to get groceries. Increased travel time can reduce the inclination to make the trip. Depending on work hours, some individuals are more likely to shop at fast food or convenience stores given how their hours fit around regular grocery stores hours.

What are the long-lasting effects ?

Impact of food choices on life expectancy

Factors such as increased sugary drink consumption, increased saturated fat consumption, and increased trans fat consumption are correlated with increased risk for diabetes, obesity, and cardiovascular disease. With these substances being cheaper and more common, food deserts have a high concentration of sugary drinks and unhealthy fats so people in these areas are at a higher risk for these adverse health conditions.

Triglycerides are a type of fat within our blood. Having high levels of triglycerides can increase the risk of heart disease. Triglycerides are developed within our bodies and we can also get it from the foods we consume. When we consume food that have high calories, fats and sugars, the extra calories and sugar that are not used are stored as triglycerides in fat cells in our bodies.

But what exactly does it mean to be food insecure?

Being food insecure means...

Your children go hungry or skip eating because you simply cannot afford enough food

You stay hungry because you don't have the luxury to get more food

You rely on community programs or food pantries to get food

You rely on low cost food because you don't have money to buy nutritious food that's more expensive

You cut the size of meals or skip eating entirely because there wasn't enough money

You constantly worry about if food will run out before you're able to get enough money to buy more



No one should have to worry about access to sufficient nutritious food.
